# Cat Nutrition and Diet Guide

Cats are obligate carnivores, meaning their diet must consist primarily of animal-based proteins. Understanding feline nutritional needs ensures your cat gets the best diet for their age, health status, and lifestyle.

## Essential Nutrients for Cats

### Protein

**Why it matters:**
- Cats require high levels of animal protein
- Essential for muscle maintenance, immune function, and growth
- Should be the primary macronutrient in a cat's diet

**Protein sources:**
- Chicken, turkey, beef, fish (animal sources preferred)
- Plant-based proteins are less complete for cats
- Quality matters — named meat sources better than "meat by-products"

**Daily requirement:**
- Adult cats: minimum 10% of diet (dry weight basis)
- Growing kittens: 30%+ of diet
- Quality food typically 30-40% protein

### Taurine

**Why it's critical:**
- Essential amino acid found exclusively in animal tissue
- Cats cannot synthesize taurine, it must come from diet
- Deficiency causes serious heart disease (dilated cardiomyopathy)
- Often the first sign of health decline

**Sources:**
- Meat, poultry, fish (all animal proteins)
- Synthetic taurine added to commercial cat foods
- Most reputable commercial foods are taurine-adequate

**Daily requirement:**
- Adult cats: ~100-200 mg/day
- Kittens: higher amounts needed for growth

### Fat

**Why it matters:**
- Provides energy and aids absorption of fat-soluble vitamins
- Contains essential fatty acids (linoleic, arachidonic)
- Important for coat health and immune function

**Healthy fat sources:**
- Fish oil (omega-3)
- Chicken fat (omega-6)
- Beef tallow

**Daily requirement:**
- 5-10% of diet minimum
- Most cats do well with 10-15%

### Vitamins and Minerals

**Key vitamins:**
- **Vitamin A**: Essential for vision, immune function (must come from animal sources for cats)
- **B vitamins**: Energy metabolism, nervous system
- **Vitamin D**: Calcium absorption and bone health
- **Vitamin E**: Antioxidant, immune support
- **Vitamin K**: Blood clotting

**Key minerals:**
- **Calcium and phosphorus**: Bone health, balanced ratio is important
- **Magnesium**: Muscle function (excess linked to urinary issues in some cats)
- **Iron**: Oxygen transport
- **Iodine**: Thyroid function
- **Zinc**: Skin and coat health

**Sources:**
- Commercial foods are typically balanced
- Whole animal diets naturally contain these
- Supplements may be needed for home-cooked diets

### Water

**Why hydration matters:**
- Cats have a naturally low thirst drive
- Many indoor cats don't drink enough
- Dehydration contributes to kidney and urinary problems
- Wet food provides 70-80% moisture vs. 10% in dry food

**Increasing hydration:**
- Offer wet food daily
- Provide running water fountains
- Multiple water bowls in different locations
- Change water daily (fresh water is more appealing)

## Types of Commercial Cat Food

### Dry Kibble

**Pros:**
- Convenient and shelf-stable
- Cost-effective
- May help slightly with dental health
- Easier portion control

**Cons:**
- Very low moisture content (10%)
- Doesn't promote hydration
- Some cats overeat dry food
- Variable quality across brands

**Choosing dry food:**
- Look for named meat as first ingredient (e.g., "chicken" not "poultry meal")
- Ensure it meets AAFCO standards
- Check for taurine content listed
- Avoid excessive fillers and grain

**Feeding:**
- Measured portions based on cat weight
- Not free-feeding (cats overeat)
- Store in cool, dry place
- Check expiration dates

### Wet or Canned Food

**Pros:**
- High moisture content (70-80%)
- Better for hydration
- Often more palatable (cats enjoy it more)
- Better for cats with urinary issues
- Some cats prefer it

**Cons:**
- More expensive than dry food
- Spoils quickly after opening (24-48 hours in fridge)
- Requires more effort to serve
- Strong odour (not ideal for all households)

**Choosing wet food:**
- Same quality standards as dry food
- Named meat sources preferred
- Check taurine content
- Check AAFCO certification
- Consider both nutrition and palatability

**Storage and feeding:**
- Keep cans sealed until opening
- Opened cans refrigerated, use within 24-48 hours
- Room-temperature food often more palatable
- Clean dishes immediately (bacteria growth)

### Semi-Moist Food

**Why generally not recommended:**
- Often higher in sugar and additives
- Less complete nutrition than wet or dry
- More expensive than dry food
- No advantage over wet food nutritionally

**Possible use:**
- Occasional treat
- Medication administration if needed
- Mixed with dry food for palatability

### Kitten-Specific Food

**Why it matters:**
- Kittens have higher caloric and nutrient needs
- Growing bodies need more protein and certain minerals
- Smaller kibble size for kitten mouths
- Appropriate fat content for growth

**When to transition:**
- Most kitten formulas good until 12 months
- Some brands have "large breed" kitten formulas for expected large cats
- Large breed cats may need specific formulas longer

**Transition strategy:**
- Gradual transition (over 7-10 days) when switching from kitten to adult food

### Senior-Specific Food

**Considerations:**
- Lower calories if overweight (metabolism slows)
- May help with joint health (added glucosamine)
- Softer texture if dental issues
- Not always necessary if senior is healthy

**When to transition:**
- Usually around age 10 years
- Earlier if health issues warrant
- Some seniors do fine on adult formula

## Alternative Diets

### Raw Diets

**Types:**
- **Commercial raw**: Pre-packaged, formulated for complete nutrition
- **Homemade raw**: Mixed at home (requires careful balance)

**Pros:**
- More natural diet for obligate carnivores
- Some cats thrive on raw diets
- High moisture content
- Minimal processing

**Cons:**
- More expensive
- Requires careful handling to prevent bacterial contamination
- Requires knowledge or professional guidance for nutritional balance
- Risk of nutritional imbalances with homemade versions
- Not suitable for immunocompromised cats or households

**If considering raw:**
- Consult with a veterinary nutritionist
- Use commercial pre-made raw for safety
- Practice strict food handling (separate utensils, surfaces)
- Not recommended for households with young children or immunocompromised people

### Home-Cooked Diets

**Pros:**
- Control over ingredients
- Can accommodate allergies
- Freshness assured

**Cons:**
- Requires veterinary nutritionist guidance
- Risk of imbalances without proper supplementation
- More time and effort required
- Cost varies widely
- Must include appropriate supplements (taurine, vitamins, minerals)

**If considering home-cooking:**
- **Must** consult a veterinary nutritionist to formulate a complete, balanced diet
- Recipes from reputable sources (not internet)
- Include necessary supplements
- Monitor cat's health and weight carefully
- Updates if cat's needs change (age, health)

### Grain-Free Diets

**Current concerns:**
- Association with heart disease (dilated cardiomyopathy) in some cases
- FDA investigating link between grain-free diets and heart issues
- May lack necessary nutrients that grains provided
- Not clearly superior to grain-inclusive diets

**Recommendation:**
- Unless your cat has a specific grain allergy, grain-inclusive foods are generally preferred
- If grain-free is necessary, consult your vet and ensure heart-healthy ingredients

## Special Diets for Health Conditions

### Urinary Health

**Diet features:**
- Controlled magnesium content
- Appropriate moisture level (wet food preferred)
- Balanced minerals to promote urinary pH

**Used for:**
- Urinary tract infections
- Bladder stones
- Struvite or calcium oxalate prevention

**Examples:**
- Prescription urinary care formulas
- Some commercial brands designed for urinary health

### Kidney Disease

**Diet features:**
- Lower protein (reduces kidney workload)
- Lower phosphorus (slows disease progression)
- Controlled sodium
- Often more palatable (encourages eating)

**Importance:**
- Slows disease progression
- Improves quality of life
- Often prescribed by vets

**Implementation:**
- Gradual transition to prescription diet
- Some cats resist dietary change with kidney disease
- Work with vet on best options

### Diabetes

**Diet management:**
- Some cats enter remission with diet change
- Higher protein, lower carbs preferred
- Weight loss important for overweight diabetic cats
- Some prescription diabetic diets available

**Success factors:**
- Transitioning to canned food increases success
- Limiting carbohydrates
- Consistent feeding times
- Regular monitoring and insulin adjustments

### Food Allergies or Sensitivities

**Common allergens:**
- Beef, fish, poultry, wheat, corn
- Proteins and carbohydrates can both cause reactions

**Diet approach:**
- Limited ingredient diet (one protein, one carb source)
- Novel protein source (rabbit, venison, duck)
- Elimination diet to identify culprit
- Prescription hydrolyzed protein diets

**Diagnosis:**
- Elimination diet trial (8-12 weeks)
- Vet can recommend appropriate diet
- Track symptoms during trial

## Reading Food Labels

### Key Information to Find

**Ingredient list:**
- First 3-5 ingredients indicate main components
- Named meats preferred over generic "meat meal"
- Avoid by-products (vague sources)
- Fillers should not be first ingredients

**Guaranteed analysis:**
- Protein percentage (minimum stated)
- Fat percentage (minimum stated)
- Fiber percentage (maximum stated)
- Moisture content
- For complete nutrition, compare to AAFCO standards

**AAFCO statement:**
- Should say "complete and balanced nutrition"
- Should specify life stage (kitten, adult, senior, all life stages)
- Evidence of actual feeding trials preferred

**Taurine content:**
- Listed as "taurine" in ingredients or guaranteed analysis
- Typically 0.1-0.3% minimum in food

## Feeding Guidelines

### Portion Sizes

**How much to feed:**
- Follow label recommendations based on cat weight
- Adjust based on cat's body condition
- Amount varies by food (calories differ between brands)
- Use measuring cup, not free-feeding

**Body condition scoring:**
- You should feel ribs easily without pressing hard
- There should be a visible waist when viewed from above
- Belly shouldn't hang or drag
- Cat should be able to groom their back end easily

### Feeding Schedule

**Adult cats:**
- Twice daily feeding (morning and evening, ~12 hours apart)
- Consistent times help with litter box habits
- Can do once daily if cat does well with it

**Kittens:**
- 8-12 weeks: 4 meals daily
- 3-6 months: 3 meals daily
- 6-12 months: 2-3 meals daily
- Over 1 year: transition to twice daily

**Senior cats:**
- Some benefit from 3-4 smaller meals
- Easier on digestion
- Monitor for weight changes

### Mixing Food Types

**Common approach:**
- Dry food at one meal, wet at another
- Helps with hydration
- Provides variety
- Maintains complete portions (dry + wet should total daily intake)

**Transition between foods:**
- Gradual transition over 7-10 days
- Mix increasing amounts of new food with old food
- Faster transitions may cause digestive upset
- Some cats are very resistant to change

## Weight Management

### Obesity in Cats

**Why it's a problem:**
- Increases risk of diabetes, arthritis, heart disease
- Reduces mobility and quality of life
- Shortens lifespan
- Worsens existing health conditions

**Causes:**
- Overeating (portions too large)
- Sedentary lifestyle
- Genetic predisposition
- Medical issues (thyroid, other conditions)

### Weight Loss Diet

**Calorie reduction:**
- Reduce portion by 15-30%
- Weight loss should be gradual (0.5-1 lb per month)
- Too-rapid weight loss risks hepatic lipidosis (fat in liver)

**Increase activity:**
- More interactive play
- Enrichment and climbing
- Puzzle feeders
- Play sessions 2-3 times daily

**Monitor progress:**
- Monthly weigh-ins
- Adjust portions as needed
- Consult vet if not progressing
- Body condition score changes (can feel ribs more easily)

**Prescription diet options:**
- Vet-prescribed weight loss foods
- Often higher protein, lower fat
- Can be more effective than home adjustment

## Supplements and Treats

### Treats

**Portion guidelines:**
- No more than 10% of daily caloric intake
- Measure treats, don't free-feed them
- Shouldn't replace meals

**Healthy treat options:**
- Cooked plain chicken or turkey
- Small amount of wet food as treat
- Commercial cat treats (check ingredients)
- Some cats enjoy cat grass

### Supplements

**When supplements might help:**
- Omega-3 for coat health or joint support
- Probiotics for digestive health (if vet recommends)
- Joint supplements (glucosamine) for arthritis

**Cautions:**
- Always consult vet before supplementing
- Some supplements interfere with medications
- Quality varies significantly
- Many supplements lack strong scientific support in cats

**Avoid:**
- Human supplements (potentially toxic)
- Unregulated products
- Excessive amounts of vitamins
