# Understanding Cat Behaviour

Cats communicate constantly through body language, vocalizations, and behaviours. Learning to interpret these signals helps you understand what your cat is feeling, needs, or wants.

## Cat Body Language

### The Tail

The tail is one of the most expressive parts of a cat's body:

**Straight up tail:**
- Confidence and friendliness
- The cat feels good and is greeting you or other cats
- May indicate interest in something

**Hooked or kinked tip:**
- Friendly greeting variation
- "Come follow me" signal
- Contentment mixed with curiosity

**Tail held horizontal or slightly down:**
- Neutral state
- Relaxed, normal activity

**Low or tucked tail:**
- Fear, anxiety, or submission
- The cat is unsure or feeling threatened
- Tucked under body indicates severe fear

**Puffed or bushy tail:**
- Fear or aggression
- The cat is trying to appear larger to predators or threats
- "Leave me alone" warning
- After fear subsides, bush indicates arousal or overstimulation

**Swishing or flicking tail:**
- Early signs of annoyance or irritation
- Can indicate focus during hunting/play
- Escalating swishing means building frustration

**Tail wrapping around you or another cat:**
- Affection and bonding
- Trust and contentment
- Social closeness

### Ears

**Ears forward:**
- Alert, interested, or confident
- The cat is focusing on something
- Generally a positive or neutral state

**Ears slightly back:**
- Mild stress or wariness
- The cat is somewhat uncomfortable but not severely threatened
- Monitoring a situation

**Ears flattened against head:**
- Fear or aggression
- The cat is defensive and may strike
- Clear warning to back off
- Associated with hissing, growling, or swatting

**Ears rotated backward (slightly back but not flat):**
- Concentrating, sometimes mixed with mild concern
- Listening to sounds behind them

**Ear flicks or twitching:**
- Mild irritation building
- The cat's patience is running out
- Usually precedes escalation to swatting or biting

### Eyes

**Normal open eyes with soft gaze:**
- Relaxed and content
- The cat is calm and engaged

**Wide eyes with dilated pupils:**
- Excitement, fear, or high arousal
- Could indicate intense play, fear, or hunting focus
- Context matters — play vs. fear looks different in body posture

**Half-closed eyes or slow blinking:**
- Trust, contentment, and relaxation
- The "cat kiss" — slow blinking back indicates affection
- Sign of deep relaxation and safety

**Staring intently (wide eyes, fixed gaze):**
- Hunting or predatory focus
- Intense interest in prey, toy, or threat
- Can escalate to pouncing

**Avoiding eye contact:**
- Some cats do this when stressed or unsure
- Others are simply calm and not intensely engaged
- Not always a sign of fear

**Narrowed eyes with slightly flattened expression:**
- Contentment and calm
- Slightly different from slow blinking

### Head and Face

**Head bunting (rubbing on you):**
- Affection and bonding
- Marking you with scent glands on head
- "You're mine" signal
- Trust and contentment

**Chin rubbing:**
- Affection and scent marking
- Often precedes purring or cuddling

**Whisker position forward:**
- Confidence and interest
- The cat is engaged with something

**Whiskers pulled back:**
- Nervousness or mild fear
- The cat is uncertain or uncomfortable

**Nose touch (booping):**
- Friendly greeting
- Affection and trust
- Desire for connection

**Hissing:**
- Warning signal
- "Stay away or I'll hurt you"
- Self-defence mechanism, not usually followed by immediate attack
- Always respect a hissing cat's space

**Growling:**
- Warning or threat
- More serious than hissing
- Often precedes aggression
- Stop interaction immediately

### Body Posture

**Relaxed, stretched out:**
- Contentment and safety
- The cat feels secure in their environment
- Not in a defensive position

**Crouched low, tucked:**
- Fear or attempt to be invisible
- The cat wants to avoid confrontation
- Feeling threatened

**Body rigid or tense:**
- Arousal, fear, or aggression
- High tension in muscles
- Escalating situation

**Play crouch (front end down, rear up):**
- Invitation to play
- Hunting position but in playful context
- Tail may be swishing
- Happy and engaged

**Arched back:**
- Classic defensive posture (back arched, fur up)
- Trying to appear larger and more threatening
- Often accompanied by hissing
- **OR** in relaxed stretching context, just stretching

**Slow blink:**
- Contentment and trust
- The cat feels safe and comfortable
- They may be about to fall asleep

**Rubbing on objects:**
- Scent marking and territory claiming
- Also feels good
- Affection when done on you

## Vocalizations

### Purring

**What it means:**
- Usually contentment and relaxation
- Also can occur during stress or pain (less common)
- Sign of trust and bonding

**Context:**
- During petting: happiness and pleasure
- When greeting you: affection and recognition
- When eating: contentment with food
- When ill: seeking comfort (watch for other signs)

### Meowing

**Normal meowing:**
- Greeting ("hello")
- Attention-seeking ("feed me," "play with me")
- Social communication with humans
- Question-like inflection can mean "what's that?"

**Excessive meowing:**
- Can indicate stress, illness, or unmet needs
- Senior cats may meow more due to cognitive changes
- Cats in heat (unfixed) meow excessively
- Thyroid issues increase vocalizations
- Consult vet if significant increase

### Chirping and Trilling

**What it means:**
- Often directed at prey (birds outside) or toys
- A hunting/excited vocalization
- Friendly greeting variation (less common)
- Mothers use it with kittens

### Yowling

**What it means:**
- Cats in heat (females) yowl persistently
- Elderly cats may yowl, especially at night
- Can indicate pain or distress
- Territory/mating call
- Consult vet if frequent

## Common Behaviours Explained

### Scratching

**Reasons cats scratch:**
- Claw maintenance (removing dead sheaths)
- Territory marking (scent glands in paws)
- Stretching muscles
- Stress relief
- Exercise

**What it means:**
- Normal, healthy, and necessary behaviour
- Not malicious or destructive
- Directing scratching to furniture means scratching posts are lacking

**Managing scratching:**
- Provide multiple scratching options
- Place scratchers near areas cat prefers to scratch
- Redirect gently when scratching furniture
- Never punish
- See [Enrichment Guide](../care/enrichment.md)

### Kneading

**What it is:**
- Rhythmic pushing with front paws alternating
- Often into soft surfaces (blankets, you, other cats)

**What it means:**
- Comfort behaviour from kittenhood (kittens knead mothers while nursing)
- Sign of contentment and trust
- Sometimes a sign of anxiety being self-soothed

**Context:**
- Normal, healthy behaviour
- Don't discourage it
- Comes with extended claws sometimes — keep nails trimmed

### Bunting (Head Rubbing)

**What it is:**
- Rubbing head or cheeks on you, other cats, objects, or furniture

**What it means:**
- Marking with scent glands
- Affection and bonding
- Claiming you as part of their family/territory
- Trust and contentment

**What to do:**
- Welcome it and reciprocate with gentle stroking
- Sign of a well-bonded cat

### Hiding

**When hiding is normal:**
- New environment or changes (hiding is fine)
- Overstimulation or stress
- During loud noises (fireworks, construction)
- When unwell (wanting to be left alone)
- Shy cats may hide frequently with trusted people

**When hiding is concerning:**
- Sudden increase in hiding (previously social cat)
- Refusing to come out for food or water
- Accompanied by other illness signs
- Combined with not using litter box
- Consult vet if sudden change

### Play Aggression

**What it looks like:**
- Pouncing, biting, grabbing
- Tail swishing rapidly
- Chasing movements
- Normal for kittens and young cats

**What it means:**
- Normal predatory play
- Not intentional aggression (no intent to harm)
- Part of learning to hunt and play

**Managing play aggression:**
- Play with wand toys (not hands)
- Wear long sleeves if getting bitten
- Stop play if it escalates
- Redirect to toys
- Don't use hands as toys
- With other cats, it's normal social play

### Chattering at Prey

**What it is:**
- Rapid teeth chattering while watching birds or prey
- Often through a window

**What it means:**
- Predatory excitement
- The cat is "hunting" (even though the prey is out of reach)
- Normal hunting instinct
- Not a sign of distress

**What to do:**
- Leave them be, it's normal
- Provide prey simulation through play instead

### Spraying (Urine Marking)

**What it is:**
- Backing up to vertical surface and spraying small amount of urine
- Different from inappropriate elimination (which happens in litter box or on floor)

**What causes it:**
- Territorial marking (especially in multi-cat households)
- Stress or anxiety
- Medical issues (consult vet first)
- Unfixed cats (spay/neuter helps)

**What to do:**
- Rule out medical causes with vet
- Address stress and provide more resources (litter boxes, territories, play)
- Clean marked areas with enzymatic cleaners
- Consider pheromone sprays (Feliway)
- Never punish

### Slow Blinking

**What it means:**
- Contentment and trust
- Cat's version of a kiss
- "You're safe, I trust you"

**How to respond:**
- Slowly blink back to show affection
- Reciprocate the trust signal
- Strengthens bonding

## Play Behaviour vs. Aggression

### Play Behaviour

- **Soft paws**: Claws may be extended but impact is soft
- **Takes turns**: Doesn't always "win"
- **Includes breaks**: Pauses during play
- **Invites more**: Comes back for more play
- **Willing participant**: Chooses to engage
- **Relaxed body**: Despite excitement, no tension

### Aggression

- **Hard impact**: Claws and teeth cause injury
- **Doesn't stop**: Won't take a break when asked
- **Escalates**: Gets more intense, not less
- **No breaks**: Continuous attempts to hurt
- **Defensive or offensive posture**: Tense, ears back, tail thrashing
- **Pupils dilated with fear or anger**: Not excitement

## Behaviour Problems and Solutions

### Excessive Vocalization

**Causes:**
- Unmet needs (food, water, litter, play)
- Medical issues (hyperthyroidism, pain, cognitive decline in seniors)
- Attention-seeking (cat learned meowing gets results)
- Separation anxiety
- Cats in heat

**Solutions:**
- Rule out medical issues with vet
- Provide more interaction and play
- Don't reward excessive meowing with attention
- Establish routine and predictability

### Destructive Scratching

**Causes:**
- Insufficient scratching posts
- Incorrectly placed posts
- Stress or anxiety
- Normal scratching being misdirected

**Solutions:**
- See [Enrichment Guide](../care/enrichment.md)
- Multiple scratchers in preferred locations
- Redirect to scratchers with praise and treats
- Don't punish

### Inappropriate Elimination (Outside Litter Box)

**Causes:**
- Medical issues (UTI, kidney disease, digestive problems)
- Insufficient litter boxes (one per cat plus one extra)
- Litter box location or cleanliness issues
- Stress or environmental changes
- Aversion to certain litter type or box

**Solutions:**
- **First**: Consult vet to rule out medical causes
- Add more litter boxes in different locations
- Scoop daily and change litter weekly
- Try different litter types
- Provide stress relief (enrichment, vertical space, hiding spots)
- Don't punish (increases stress and problem)
