# Cat Adoption Checklist

Before bringing a new cat home, preparation is essential. A well-prepared environment reduces stress for both the cat and household, leading to a smoother transition and better relationship.

## Pre-Adoption Planning

### Choose a Veterinarian
- Research local veterinary clinics before adoption
- Confirm hours, emergency services, and pricing
- Ask about preventive care packages and kitten/senior wellness plans
- Schedule a first appointment within the first week of adoption

### Prepare Your Budget
- **First-year costs**: Vet exams, vaccines, spay/neuter, supplies ($500-$1,000+)
- **Ongoing costs**: Food, litter, vet care, toys ($30-$50/month typical)
- **Emergency fund**: Set aside $500-$1,000 for unexpected health issues

### Assess Your Living Situation
- Do you have permission to keep a cat? (Check lease or HOA rules)
- Can you commit to 15-20 years? (Cat lifespans)
- Do you have time for daily care and play?
- Can you handle the responsibility and costs?

## Essential Supplies

Have these items ready before bringing your cat home:

### Litter & Bathroom
- Litter box (or two — aim for one per cat plus one extra)
- Litter (unscented works well initially; transition slowly if needed)
- Litter scoop
- Waste bags or disposal system

### Feeding & Hydration
- Food and water bowls (separate, not side-by-side)
- Cat food appropriate for age (kitten, adult, or senior)
- Fresh water source (fountain optional but helpful)

### Comfort & Safety
- Bed or blanket for comfort
- Hiding spots (cat tunnel, covered bed, or cardboard box)
- Scratching post or scratch pad
- Toys (wand toys, balls, mice, puzzle toys)

### Grooming & Health
- Nail clippers (cat-specific)
- Brush or comb
- Pet first-aid kit
- Carrier (for vet visits and safety)

## Home Safety Preparation

### Remove Hazards
- **Toxic plants**: Lilies, oleander, poinsettia, philodendron, dieffenbachia, sago palm
- **Toxic foods**: Onions, garlic, grapes, raisins, chocolate, xylitol, raw eggs
- **Medications**: Store safely out of reach
- **Chemicals**: Secure cleaning products, pesticides, antifreeze
- **Small objects**: Remove choking hazards like string, rubber bands, small toys

### Secure the Environment
- Ensure windows and screens are secure
- Block access to balconies or high-risk areas
- Check for gaps behind appliances where cats could get stuck
- Remove or secure loose electrical cords
- Keep doors closed to prevent escapes

### Create Designated Spaces
- **Safe room**: A quiet bedroom or bathroom where the cat can stay initially
- **Litter location**: Quiet area away from food and water
- **High perches**: Shelves or cat trees for climbing and observation

## The First Days at Home

### Start Small (The Safe Room)
1. Place the cat in a quiet room with litter, food, water, bed, and toys
2. Keep the door closed initially — let the cat explore this room for 3-7 days
3. Spend time sitting quietly nearby so the cat adjusts to your presence
4. Don't force interaction; let the cat approach you
5. Gradually open the door to other rooms once the cat seems confident

### Monitor Early Behaviour
- It's normal for a new cat to hide for 24-48 hours
- If hiding lasts longer than a week, consult your vet
- Eating, drinking, and using the litter box are good signs of adjustment
- Some cats are immediately social; others need weeks to warm up

### Schedule the First Vet Visit
- Within the first week or two of adoption
- Discuss flea, tick, and parasite prevention
- Ask about spay/neuter if not already done
- Get baseline health information

## Introducing to Resident Pets

### Introducing to Other Cats
1. **Scent swapping** (1-2 weeks): Place the new cat's bedding where resident cats sleep and vice versa
2. **Feeding separately**: Feed on opposite sides of the door so they associate each other with positive experiences
3. **Visual introduction**: Crack the door slightly or use a baby gate so they can see each other
4. **Short supervised meetings**: Gradually allow face-to-face contact with close supervision
5. **Full integration**: Once they're comfortable, grant full household access

**Patience is key** — rushing this can lead to conflict. Some cats take weeks or months to become comfortable with each other.

### Introducing to Dogs
1. Ensure the cat has high perches and hiding spots the dog cannot access
2. Keep them separated initially — feed on opposite sides of a door
3. Let them sniff under the door to get used to each other's scent
4. Use a baby gate so they can see each other from a distance
5. Supervise all interactions until you're confident they're safe
6. Reward calm behaviour in the dog with treats and praise

## Setting Up for Success

### First Week Checklist
- [ ] Vet appointment scheduled
- [ ] All supplies purchased and set up
- [ ] Home hazards removed
- [ ] Safe room prepared
- [ ] Other pets prepared (if applicable)
- [ ] Time set aside for attention and observation
- [ ] Backup plan if problems arise

### Managing Expectations
- Some cats adjust in days; others take weeks
- Personality changes are normal — shy cats may become social, or vice versa
- Bonding deepens over time; be patient
- Every cat is different — what works for one may not work for another

### Resources
- Contact your vet with questions about diet, behaviour, or health
- Local animal shelters often offer adoption support
- Cat behaviour websites and books provide additional guidance
