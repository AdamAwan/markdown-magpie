# Introduction Guide for New Cats

A successful introduction to your home determines whether a cat becomes a confident, integrated family member. This guide covers the critical first weeks after adoption.

## The Transition Process

### Day 1: Arrival

**Before the cat arrives:**
- Set up the safe room completely (litter, food, water, bed, toys, hiding spots)
- Remove other household members temporarily if they're excitable
- Have a carrier ready and position it in the safe room

**When the cat arrives:**
1. Bring the cat directly to the safe room in the carrier
2. Open the carrier and let the cat come out on their own — don't pull them out
3. Show the cat the litter box location
4. Place the cat gently near food and water bowls
5. Sit quietly nearby but don't hover or stare
6. Leave the room and let the cat settle for several hours

**First hours expectations:**
- The cat may hide under furniture or in a dark corner
- Some cats will explore immediately; others will freeze
- Hiding is completely normal and not a sign of a problem
- Do NOT pull the cat out of hiding to "help" them settle

### Days 2-7: Initial Adjustment

**Daily routine:**
- Clean the litter box twice daily
- Refresh food and water
- Spend 30-60 minutes in the room reading, working quietly, or relaxing
- Don't force interaction; let the cat approach you
- Talk in a calm, quiet voice
- Offer slow blinks if the cat looks at you (this is a friendly signal)

**Signs of adjustment:**
- Exploring the room
- Using the litter box
- Eating and drinking consistently
- Relaxed body posture
- Initiating contact (rubbing on you, approaching)

**If the cat isn't adjusting:**
- Some cats take longer — up to 2 weeks is normal
- Ensure the room is quiet and relatively undisturbed
- Check that food and water are fresh and accessible
- If the cat stops eating, consult your vet immediately

### Week 2: Gradual Expansion

**Transition to the hallway:**
1. Once the cat seems confident in the safe room, crack the door open slightly
2. Let the cat explore the hallway at their own pace — don't push them
3. Still keep them confined to a small area; use baby gates if needed
4. Place a litter box in the hallway in addition to the one in the safe room
5. Spend time in the hallway so the cat can continue getting used to you

**Gradual freedom:**
- Open doors to adjacent rooms after a few days
- Don't force the cat through open doors
- Set up additional litter boxes, water, and food in different areas
- The cat may still retreat to the safe room — that's fine

### Week 3+: Full Home Integration

**Expanding access:**
- Gradually allow access to more rooms
- The cat may still prefer certain areas — that's normal
- Continue using multiple litter boxes (aim for one per cat plus one extra)

**Establishing routine:**
- Feed at the same times daily
- Play at consistent times
- Spend interactive time together
- Maintain a calm household environment

## Introducing to Resident Cats

### Pre-Introduction Preparation

**Scent swapping (1-2 weeks before or parallel to above):**
1. Place the new cat's bedding in areas where resident cats sleep
2. Place resident cats' bedding in the new cat's safe room
3. Swap towels that have their scents on them
4. This allows them to get used to each other's scent without face-to-face meetings
5. Rub a towel on one cat and place it near the other cat's food bowl (optional — some recommend against this)

**Separate feeding:**
- Feed on opposite sides of the door between rooms
- This creates positive associations — they link each other's presence with food
- Do this for at least 1-2 weeks before visual introductions

### Visual Introduction

**Step 1: Door opening (Week 2-3)**
1. Crack the door between rooms just an inch or two
2. Let cats observe each other from a distance
3. Don't force them to the door; let them approach if curious
4. Repeat this for several days, gradually widening the gap
5. Close the door if either cat becomes very stressed or aggressive

**Step 2: Baby gate barrier (Week 3-4)**
1. Once they've tolerated the door opening, use a baby gate between rooms
2. This allows visual and scent contact without direct access
3. Supervise their interactions at the gate
4. Continue for 1-2 weeks until both cats seem calm and curious

**Step 3: Supervised meetings (Week 4+)**
1. Set up a neutral space, ideally not one resident cat's territory
2. Have an escape route so either cat can leave
3. Start with 10-15 minute meetings
4. Keep sessions short and positive
5. Reward calm behaviour with treats and praise
6. Gradually extend meeting duration

### Managing the Introduction Timeline

| Timeline | Activity |
|----------|----------|
| Weeks 1-2 | Scent swapping, separate feeding, adjusting to safe room |
| Weeks 2-3 | Door cracking, continued scent exposure |
| Weeks 3-4 | Baby gate viewing, visual introduction |
| Week 4+ | Supervised meetings in neutral space |
| Week 6-12 | Gradual full-time integration |

**Important notes:**
- Every cat pair is different — some take weeks, others take months
- If there's hissing or swatting, that's normal cat communication; don't rush integration
- Avoid forced interaction or punishment
- If serious fighting occurs, separate and slow down the process

### Signs of Successful Integration

- Cats eat calmly in the same area (even if not immediately next to each other)
- They groom themselves without tension in shared space
- They sleep in the same room without stalking each other
- Relaxed body posture and natural movement around each other
- Optional: Playing together or grooming each other

## Introducing to Dogs

### Pre-Introduction (1-2 weeks)

**Prepare the dog:**
- Dogs should know basic commands (sit, stay, leave it)
- Practice calm behaviour around the door where the cat will be
- Reward calm, quiet behaviour extensively

**Prepare the cat:**
- Ensure the cat has cat-only spaces with high perches
- Set up an escape route; cats need to feel safe
- Place litter box and resources where the dog cannot block access

**Separate living:**
- Keep cat and dog in different areas initially
- Feed on opposite sides of a door
- Let them sniff under the door
- This is crucial for safety

### First Meetings

**Initial visual introduction:**
1. Use a baby gate or keep the dog on a lead
2. Keep the dog calm and sitting or lying down
3. Reward any calm, disinterested behaviour
4. Let the cat observe from a distance (high perch is ideal)
5. Keep the meeting brief (5-10 minutes)

**Gradual exposure:**
- Repeat short meetings daily
- Gradually increase time as both animals stay calm
- Remove the lead only when you're certain the dog won't chase
- Always supervise

**Important safety rules:**
- Never leave them unsupervised until absolutely certain they're safe
- Dog play instinct can look like predatory behaviour to a cat
- Some dog breeds have high prey drives — be extra cautious
- Cat's escape routes must always be available

### What Success Looks Like

- Dog lies calmly while cat is present
- Cat moves about confidently without hiding constantly
- Both animals can coexist in the same room peacefully
- Dog ignores the cat or shows calm curiosity
- Cat doesn't feel threatened enough to constantly hide

## Special Situations

### Senior Cats
- Adjust slower; may need 4+ weeks to settle
- Ensure litter box is easily accessible
- Monitor closely for stress-related health issues
- They may prefer quiet, undisturbed spaces

### Kittens
- Adjust quickly but need close monitoring
- More prone to accidents during the transition
- Require more play and interaction
- May seem overwhelmed by larger environments

### Cats with Behaviour Issues
- If the cat was previously abused, allow extra time
- Consult a veterinary behaviourist if serious issues persist
- Some cats have permanent shyness — that's okay
- Never force a cat into situations they fear

### Multi-Cat Households
- Introduce one cat at a time if adding multiple cats
- Allow 2-3 weeks between introductions if needed
- Each pairing needs individual attention

## When to Call Your Vet

- Cat hasn't eaten in 24+ hours
- Litter box isn't being used
- Signs of stress-related illness (vomiting, diarrhoea)
- Extreme behaviour (won't stop hiding, extreme aggression)
- Health concerns (discharge, lethargy, limping)

## Timeline Summary

**Week 1:** Safe room adjustment  
**Weeks 2-3:** Gradual hallway/home expansion  
**Weeks 3-4:** Resident cat introductions begin  
**Weeks 4-8:** Supervised meetings if applicable  
**Weeks 8-12:** Full integration into household  
**Months 3+:** Stable routine and deep bonding
