# Cat Enrichment Guide

Environmental enrichment and play are essential for cat physical health, mental wellbeing, and behaviour. This guide covers how to create an enriched environment that keeps cats engaged, active, and happy.

## Why Enrichment Matters

### The Cost of Boredom

Under-stimulated cats often develop:
- Destructive behaviour (scratching furniture, knocking items off shelves)
- Obesity and related health problems
- Stress-related illness (overgrooming, inappropriate elimination)
- Excessive meowing or attention-seeking
- Aggression or excessive play-biting

### Benefits of Proper Enrichment

- Increased activity and better fitness
- Reduced stress and anxiety
- Appropriate outlet for natural behaviours
- Better sleep and digestion
- Fewer behaviour problems
- Longer, healthier life

## Understanding Cat Instincts

Cats are hunters with natural drives to:
- **Hunt and stalk** prey
- **Explore** territory
- **Climb** and survey from height
- **Hide** and feel safe
- **Mark** territory through scratching and scent
- **Scratch** to maintain claws and stretch

Enrichment allows these natural behaviours in appropriate ways.

## Types of Enrichment

### Feeding-Based Enrichment

**Puzzle feeders and food toys:**
- Slow eating and aid digestion
- Engage problem-solving instincts
- Provide mental stimulation
- Can prevent obesity

**Types:**
- **Treat balls**: Food dispenses as the cat rolls and paws the toy
- **Multi-level puzzles**: Multiple steps to reach food
- **Lick mats**: Spread wet food, freeze, and cats lick to eat
- **Slow feeders**: Bowls with obstacles to slow eating

**How to use:**
- Start with easy puzzles and gradually increase difficulty
- Not all cats enjoy puzzles — watch for frustration
- Use 10-25% of daily food in puzzle feeders
- Rotate types to maintain interest

### Visual Enrichment

**Window perches:**
- Most important enrichment for indoor cats
- Provide views of birds, squirrels, weather, and passing people
- Install secure shelves or window hammocks
- Multiple windows if possible

**Cat TV and videos:**
- Videos of birds, squirrels, or fish
- Some cats watch intently; others ignore them
- Useful on bad weather days
- Not a replacement for real windows

**Outdoor viewing:**
- If safe, allow supervised outdoor time in a catio or on a harness
- Birds and wildlife provide natural stimulation
- Sunlight benefits overall health

### Scent Enrichment

**Catnip:**
- Dried catnip in toys or sprinkled on scratchers
- Most cats respond with interest and play
- Some cats (especially kittens under 6 months) don't respond
- Effects wear off if used constantly — rotate in and out

**Silvervine and valerian:**
- Alternative to catnip with similar effects
- Some cats prefer these over catnip
- Rotate between options

**Pheromone products:**
- Feliway mimics calming cat pheromones
- Helpful for anxious or stressed cats
- Can reduce urine marking and aggression

**Scent rubbing:**
- Let your cat rub on you (they love this)
- Provides comfort and bonding
- Safe and natural enrichment

**Scent toys:**
- Rub toys on other cats, plants, or areas
- Fresh scents stimulate interest
- Rotate scent sources for novelty

### Physical Enrichment

#### Climbing Structures

**Cat trees and shelves:**
- Vertical territory satisfies climbing instinct
- Provides elevated vantage points (cats feel safer above)
- Multi-level trees suit multiple cats
- Wall-mounted shelves save space

**Ideas:**
- Ladder shelves along walls
- Window shelves for climbing plus viewing
- Tree structures with hiding spots and multiple levels
- Ensure stability — unstable trees cause injury

**Maintenance:**
- Check for damage regularly
- Loose posts or wobbly platforms are dangerous
- Cover with new carpet or sisal when worn

#### Scratching Posts and Pads

**Why scratching is essential:**
- Maintains claw health and removes dead sheaths
- Marks territory with scent glands on paws
- Stretches muscles
- Provides stress relief

**Placement strategy:**
- Multiple posts in different areas (not just one in a corner)
- Near sleeping areas and main activity zones
- Near furniture cats are likely to scratch

**Types:**
- **Vertical posts**: Stretched scratching (cats stretch fully while scratching)
- **Horizontal pads**: Crouched scratching (some cats prefer this)
- **Angled scratchers**: Different angle options
- **Corrugated cardboard**: Inexpensive and replaceable

**Materials:**
- Sisal rope or sisal fabric: Durable and preferred by most cats
- Corrugated cardboard: Satisfying for many cats
- Wood: Some cats prefer this
- Carpet: Avoid — teaches cats it's okay to scratch carpet at home

**Encouraging use:**
- Place posts where cats like to scratch (near furniture, after waking)
- Rub catnip on posts to attract interest
- Reward with praise and treats when you see scratching
- Provide a post larger than the cat can fully stretch across

#### Jumping and Perching

**Why cats jump:**
- Exercise and muscle building
- Surveying territory from height
- Feeling safe from perceived threats
- Natural behaviour

**Safe jumping:**
- Provide stable platforms and landing spots
- Furniture should be sturdy enough to handle impact
- Senior and obese cats may need ramps or lower options
- Watch for injuries from falls

### Social Enrichment

**Interactive play with humans:**
- Wand toys that mimic prey (string, feathers, ribbons)
- 10-15 minutes per session, 2+ times daily
- Let the cat "catch" the toy occasionally
- Stop play before the cat becomes frustrated or tired

**Play with other cats:**
- Multi-cat households provide social enrichment
- Young cats often play more actively together
- Supervise play to ensure it doesn't escalate to fighting
- Older cats may prefer to observe rather than actively play

**Talking and interaction:**
- Regular conversation and attention bond you
- Respond when your cat meows or seeks attention
- Many cats enjoy being talked to during routine activities

### Sensory Enrichment

**Safe plants:**
- Cats enjoy chewing some plants
- **Safe plants**: Cat grass, spider plant, Boston fern
- **Toxic plants**: Lilies, dieffenbachia, oleander, poinsettia (research before adding)
- Place in accessible spots

**Water enrichment:**
- Some cats enjoy running water (fountains)
- Others like playing in shallow water dishes
- Water bowls can provide tactile enrichment
- Change water frequently

**Textures:**
- Different surfaces: carpet, wood, tile, grass
- Some cats enjoy lying on different materials
- Rotating textures can provide novelty

## Enrichment by Age and Lifestyle

### Indoor Cats

**Need more structured enrichment:**
- All enrichment types above are essential
- Window access is critical
- Regular interactive play from you
- Puzzle feeders and toys
- Safe plants to explore
- Multiple levels and hiding spots

### Outdoor or Indoor-Outdoor Cats

**Natural enrichment:**
- Outdoor environment provides constant stimulation
- Still benefit from interactive play with humans
- Window perches inside
- Safe spaces to retreat to

### Senior Cats (10+ years)

**Adapted enrichment:**
- Lower climbing options or ramps
- Puzzle feeders with large or easy-access food
- Gentle interactive play
- Comfortable perches and hiding spots
- Softer play (wand toys with less vigorous movement)
- Pain-free scratching (low scratchers)

### Kittens

**High-energy enrichment:**
- Lots of interactive play (multiple times daily)
- Safe climbing (supervised if needed)
- Scratching posts for claw maintenance
- Solo toys for independent play
- Water fountains (some kittens enjoy water)
- Rotate toys frequently

## Creating an Enriched Environment: A Room-by-Room Guide

### Living Room
- Window perches
- Cat tree or shelf
- Wand toys for interactive play
- Puzzle feeder mat
- Comfortable perch or bed

### Bedroom
- High perch or shelf
- Scratching post
- Hidden spot or tunnel
- Quiet retreat space

### Kitchen
- Near window if possible
- Puzzle feeders and treat toys
- Wand toys for pre-meal play

### Bathroom
- Cat grass
- Water fountain
- Comfortable perch

### Stairs or Hallways
- Climbing surfaces
- Passage routes between spaces
- Wall-mounted shelves creating highways

## Enrichment Rotation and Novelty

**Rotate toys:**
- Store toys not currently in use
- Bring out different toys weekly
- A "new" toy is often an old toy forgotten
- This maintains interest and reduces boredom

**Seasonal changes:**
- Window entertainment changes seasonally (different wildlife)
- Indoor activities increase in bad weather
- Outdoor access varies

**DIY enrichment:**
- Crumpled paper or paper bags
- Cardboard boxes (cut holes for exploration)
- Paper towel tubes stuffed with shredded paper
- Ice cubes or water play (some cats enjoy)
- Safe household items

## Monitoring and Adjusting

**Signs enrichment is working:**
- Cat is active and playful
- Good appetite and sleep
- Calm, relaxed behaviour
- No destructive behaviour
- Engaged with environment

**Signs enrichment is inadequate:**
- Excessive vocalization or attention-seeking
- Destructive scratching on furniture
- Excessive grooming or hair loss
- Overweight or sedentary
- Stress-related behaviours (urine marking, aggression)

**Adjusting enrichment:**
- Watch what your cat enjoys most
- Adapt to their age and energy level
- Keep rotating and trying new things
- What works for one cat won't work for another
