# Cat Health and Monitoring

Cats are experts at hiding illness, which makes regular monitoring essential. Understanding normal behaviour and recognising subtle changes can help catch health problems early when they're easier and cheaper to treat.

## Understanding Cat Behaviour Baselines

Before you can notice changes, establish what's normal for your cat:

### Normal Patterns

**Eating and drinking:**
- Consistent appetite
- Eats meals at regular times
- Drinks water throughout the day
- Water intake increases in summer or with dry food diet

**Litter box:**
- Consistent frequency (typically 1-2 times daily for urine, 1 time daily for feces)
- Normal urine colour (pale to medium yellow)
- Normal stool consistency (firm, not loose or hard)
- Uses litter box without straining

**Activity level:**
- Sleep patterns (cats sleep 12-16 hours daily — normal)
- Active play at typical times
- Consistent energy level
- Normal grooming frequency

**Appearance:**
- Clear, bright eyes
- Clean ears
- Normal breathing pattern
- Good body condition (can feel ribs without pressing hard, visible waist)
- Smooth coat

**Behaviour:**
- Consistent personality
- Normal vocalization (purring, meowing)
- Greeting you at door or seeking attention as usual
- Interest in environment

## Warning Signs: When to Contact Your Vet

### Seek Immediate Veterinary Care (Emergency)

These signs warrant emergency vet care immediately:

**Breathing problems:**
- Difficulty breathing, panting, rapid breathing at rest
- Open-mouth breathing (not typical for cats)
- Wheezing or gasping

**Severe symptoms:**
- Inability to urinate or straining in litter box
- Inability to defecate (constipation)
- Repeated vomiting (especially if unable to keep water down)
- Collapse, seizures, or loss of consciousness
- Severe lethargy or unresponsiveness
- Inability to walk or sudden paralysis

**Severe pain or trauma:**
- Severe pain (crying, aggression when touched, hiding)
- Suspected poisoning (ate toxic substance)
- Hit by car or severe trauma
- Pale gums (indicates blood loss or circulation problem)

### Schedule a Vet Appointment (Within 24-48 Hours)

**Moderate symptoms that need professional attention:**
- Vomiting (even if not constant)
- Diarrhoea for more than 24 hours
- Straining or difficulty using litter box
- Not eating for 24+ hours
- Excessive drinking or urination
- Discharge from eyes, nose, or genital area
- Lumps, bumps, or skin abnormalities
- Limping or lameness
- Squinting or eye redness
- Difficulty chewing or dropping food from mouth
- Hair loss or excessive itching
- Behaviour changes (aggression, hiding, vocalization changes)

## Common Health Issues and Monitoring

### Urinary Tract Issues

**Why it matters:**
- Common in cats, especially males
- Can block and become life-threatening
- Prevention and early treatment are important

**Warning signs:**
- Straining to urinate or inability to urinate (emergency)
- Frequent small urinations
- Urinating outside the litter box
- Bloody or cloudy urine
- Crying or pain while urinating

**Prevention:**
- Adequate hydration (wet food helps)
- Multiple litter boxes
- Clean litter boxes
- Regular vet checkups

### Kidney Disease

**Why it matters:**
- Common in senior cats
- Chronic condition, not curable but manageable
- Early detection improves quality of life

**Warning signs:**
- Increased thirst and urination
- Weight loss
- Vomiting
- Bad breath
- Lethargy
- Decreased appetite

**Monitoring:**
- Senior cats (10+) need twice-yearly checkups
- Regular bloodwork detects early kidney disease
- Adequate hydration helps slow progression

### Diabetes

**Why it matters:**
- More common in overweight cats
- Manageable with diet and sometimes insulin
- Early detection allows dietary management

**Warning signs:**
- Increased thirst and urination
- Weight loss despite good appetite
- Increased appetite
- Lethargy
- Weak hind legs

**Prevention:**
- Maintain healthy weight
- Feed measured portions
- Regular exercise through play

### Hyperthyroidism

**Why it matters:**
- Common in senior cats
- Usually treatable
- If untreated, can cause heart problems

**Warning signs:**
- Increased appetite but weight loss
- Increased thirst and urination
- Overactivity or restlessness
- Vomiting
- Diarrhoea
- Rough coat

**Diagnosis:**
- Blood test can confirm
- More common in cats 10+ years old
- Regular senior checkups often catch this

### Obesity

**Why it's a problem:**
- Increases risk of diabetes, arthritis, heart disease, joint problems
- Reduces lifespan
- Worsens existing conditions

**Management:**
- Measure portions using food labels
- Weigh your cat monthly
- Increase interactive play
- Use puzzle feeders
- Consult vet for weight loss plans

### Dental Disease

**Why it matters:**
- Causes pain and affects eating
- Affects overall health and organs
- Preventable through regular care

**Warning signs:**
- Bad breath (worse than normal "cat breath")
- Difficulty eating or chewing
- Drooling
- Swelling around mouth
- Loose or missing teeth

**Prevention:**
- Brush teeth several times per week
- Dental-friendly food options
- Professional cleaning when needed

### Hairballs

**Normal vs. concerning:**
- Occasional hairballs are normal
- Frequent hairballs (more than 1-2 monthly) suggest a problem
- Hairballs should come up relatively easily

**Warning signs of problems:**
- Frequent hairball attempts without producing anything (could indicate blockage)
- Vomiting without hairballs
- Loss of appetite
- Constipation
- Lethargy

**Management:**
- Brushing regularly reduces ingested hair
- Increased hydration helps move hairballs through
- Some foods are formulated to help with hairballs
- Consult vet if frequent

## Preventive Care Schedule

### Kittens (Under 1 Year)

| Age | Care |
|-----|------|
| 6-8 weeks | First vet visit, vaccines, flea/tick prevention |
| 10-12 weeks | Second vaccine dose, flea/tick prevention |
| 14-16 weeks | Final vaccine dose, flea/tick prevention, test for FeLV/FIV |
| 4-6 months | Spay/neuter discussion |
| After 6 months | Spay/neuter (typically 6-8 months), continue prevention |

### Adult Cats (1-10 Years)

| Frequency | Care |
|-----------|------|
| Annually | Wellness exam, vaccines if needed, bloodwork if requested |
| Annually | Flea, tick, parasite prevention (year-round) |
| As needed | Dental care, weight management |

### Senior Cats (10+ Years)

| Frequency | Care |
|-----------|------|
| Twice yearly | Wellness exams (more frequent than younger cats) |
| Twice yearly | Bloodwork and urinalysis (screens for kidney disease, thyroid, etc.) |
| Annually or as-needed | Dental care, pain management |
| Annually | Flea/tick/parasite prevention (continue even in older cats) |

## At-Home Monitoring

### Monthly Cat Health Check

Perform this simple check monthly:

1. **Eyes**: Clear, bright, no discharge or redness
2. **Ears**: Clean, no discharge, normal colour inside
3. **Nose**: Moist, clear (slight discharge is normal after sleep)
4. **Mouth**: Pink gums, no bad smell, teeth visible without looseness
5. **Body**: Feel ribs easily without pressing hard, no lumps or bumps
6. **Skin and coat**: Smooth coat, no bare patches or excessive itching
7. **Paws**: Check between toes, nails not overgrown
8. **Weight**: Compare to last month, no sudden changes
9. **Movement**: Walking and jumping normally
10. **Litter box**: Normal frequency, colour, and consistency

### Health Journal

Consider keeping simple notes:
- Appetite changes
- Activity level changes
- Litter box changes
- Vet visit summaries
- Vaccination dates
- Diet changes

This helps you identify patterns and provides info for your vet.

## Common Non-Emergency Issues

### Sneezing and Mild Cough

**Often caused by:**
- Upper respiratory infection (viral, usually resolves in 7-10 days)
- Dust or environmental irritants
- Mild asthma or allergies

**Monitor for:**
- If it persists beyond 10 days
- If accompanied by discharge or breathing difficulty
- If appetite changes
- Contact vet if worsening

### Minor Digestive Upset

**Occasional diarrhoea (24 hours):**
- Often resolves on its own
- Ensure hydration
- Monitor food quality
- Switch to bland diet if continues

**When to contact vet:**
- Diarrhoea lasts more than 24 hours
- Blood in stool
- Cat seems ill or lethargic
- Vomiting accompanies diarrhoea

### Mild Limping

**Temporary causes:**
- Minor injury from play
- Splinter in paw
- Muscle soreness

**Monitor for improvement:**
- Most resolve within 24-48 hours
- If worsens or persists, contact vet
- Severely limiting mobility warrants urgent vet visit

## Medication Management

**When your cat needs medication:**
- Use exactly as prescribed
- Complete full course even if cat seems better
- Store as instructed (refrigeration, away from light)
- Keep medication list updated

**Giving medications:**
- Tablets can go in pill pockets, wet food, or given directly
- Liquids can be mixed into wet food or given with syringe
- Ask your vet to demonstrate techniques
- Never hide medication so your cat doesn't know what's happening

## End-of-Life Considerations

**When a cat is suffering:**
- Quality of life is the priority
- Your vet can discuss end-of-life options
- Euthanasia is a kind option when suffering is unavoidable
- Grief is normal

**Resources:**
- Discuss end-of-life plans with your vet
- Pet hospice services exist in some areas
- Support groups exist for pet loss
